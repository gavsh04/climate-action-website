🌊 Ocean Health & Climate Action

How our oceans are changing due to climate change — and what we can do about it.

Introduction: Why Oceans Matter

Oceans cover more than 70% of the Earth's surface and are fundamental to all life on our planet. They produce over half of the world's oxygen, regulate global temperature, and are home to approximately 80% of all living species on Earth. For billions of people, oceans are also a primary source of food and livelihood.

Yet despite their critical importance, our oceans are under unprecedented threat from climate change. Rising temperatures, ocean acidification, pollution, and overfishing are pushing marine ecosystems to breaking point. As a student and future professional, understanding ocean health is not just a scientific concern — it is a deeply human one.

📊 Key Fact: Oceans have absorbed about 90% of the excess heat generated by the greenhouse effect since industrialisation began. This is dramatically altering marine biodiversity and weather patterns worldwide.

Threats Facing Our Oceans

Ocean Acidification

When carbon dioxide (CO₂) is released into the atmosphere, a significant portion is absorbed by the ocean. This triggers a chemical reaction that increases acidity levels in seawater. Since the Industrial Revolution, ocean acidity has increased by about 26%. This threatens shellfish, corals, and the entire marine food web that depends on calcium carbonate structures.

Ocean Warming

The top layers of the ocean have been warming at an accelerating rate. Warmer water holds less oxygen, disrupting habitats for fish and marine mammals. It also intensifies hurricanes and typhoons, affects rainfall patterns on land, and alters the ocean currents that millions of species — and weather systems — depend on.

Plastic Pollution

Every year, an estimated 8 million tonnes of plastic waste enters the ocean. This waste fragments into microplastics that are ingested by sea creatures at every level of the food chain — including fish that end up on our plates. Coastal communities, especially in developing nations, face the most direct impacts of this crisis.

📊 Key Fact: There are now more pieces of microplastic in the ocean than there are stars in the Milky Way galaxy. Most of this plastic comes from single-use consumer packaging.

The Coral Reef Crisis

Coral reefs are often called the rainforests of the sea. Covering less than 1% of the ocean floor, they support around 25% of all marine species. They also protect coastlines from storm surges and generate billions of pounds in tourism and fishing income for communities worldwide.

Rising ocean temperatures cause coral bleaching — a process where stressed corals expel the symbiotic algae that give them colour and nutrition. If temperatures remain elevated for too long, the coral dies. Since the 1950s, the world has lost roughly 50% of its coral reefs, and projections suggest that 90% could disappear by 2050 if global warming continues at its current rate.

What Is Being Done?

Scientists and local communities are working on coral restoration projects, breeding heat-resistant coral strains, and establishing Marine Protected Areas (MPAs). However, these efforts can only succeed if the root cause — carbon emissions — is meaningfully reduced on a global scale.

Rising Sea Levels

Global sea levels are rising at a rate of approximately 3.6 mm per year, driven by the melting of glaciers and ice sheets, and the thermal expansion of warming seawater. Since 1900, sea levels have risen by about 20 cm on average globally, but this is not evenly distributed — some regions experience far greater increases.

For small island nations in the Pacific and Indian Oceans — places like Tuvalu and the Maldives — sea level rise is an existential threat. Entire nations risk becoming uninhabitable within decades. Closer to home, coastal cities such as London, Jakarta, and Miami face increased flooding risks that will require massive infrastructure investment.

📊 Key Fact: If the Antarctic ice sheet were to fully melt, global sea levels could rise by an estimated 60 metres — enough to submerge most of the world's major coastal cities.

Actions You Can Take

Protecting the ocean is not only the responsibility of governments and corporations. As a student and citizen, there are many meaningful actions you can take today to reduce your impact on marine environments and advocate for systemic change.

Reduce Your Plastic Use

Carry a reusable water bottle, coffee cup, and shopping bag. Refuse single-use plastics wherever possible. Choose products with minimal or recyclable packaging. These small daily choices, at scale, significantly reduce the flow of plastic into our oceans.

Lower Your Carbon Footprint

Since ocean acidification and warming are directly driven by CO₂ emissions, reducing your personal carbon footprint has a direct benefit for marine health. Eat less meat, use public transport, fly less frequently, and choose energy-efficient appliances and suppliers.

Support Marine Conservation

Donate to or volunteer with organisations dedicated to ocean health. Participate in local beach clean-up events. Sign petitions for stronger marine protection laws and greater Marine Protected Area coverage. Educate others — sharing knowledge is one of the most powerful tools we have.

Choose Sustainable Seafood

Look for certified sustainable seafood labels (such as MSC) when shopping. Unsustainable fishing practices devastate fish populations and destroy the seabed. Your purchasing decisions send signals to supermarkets and suppliers about what consumers value.
