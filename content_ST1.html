What is Renewable Energy?

Renewable energy comes from natural sources that are constantly replenished — sources like sunlight, wind, rain, tides, waves, and geothermal heat. Unlike fossil fuels, which took millions of years to form and release carbon dioxide when burned, renewables generate power with minimal greenhouse gas emissions.

The International Energy Agency (IEA) defines renewable energy as energy derived from natural processes that are replenished at a rate that is equal to or faster than the rate at which they are consumed. This distinction is critical: the planet cannot run out of sunlight or wind the way it can run out of coal or oil.

Solar panels on a rooftop, converting sunlight directly into usable electricity.

Primary Energy Sources

Several mature technologies now allow us to harness renewable resources at scale. Each has its own strengths, ideal geography, and trade-offs.

☀️

Solar Power

Photovoltaic (PV) panels convert sunlight into electricity using the photoelectric effect. Solar is now the cheapest source of new electricity in history in many parts of the world, making it one of the fastest-growing energy technologies globally.

💨

Wind Energy

Wind turbines capture kinetic energy from moving air and convert it into electricity. Offshore wind farms benefit from stronger, more consistent winds, while onshore turbines are increasingly cost-competitive with conventional power plants.

🌍

Geothermal Energy

Heat from deep within the Earth — produced by the decay of radioactive materials — is harnessed via steam turbines. Countries like Iceland and Kenya already meet a significant portion of their electricity needs through geothermal sources.

💧

Hydropower

The largest single source of renewable electricity globally, hydropower uses the flow of water through turbines to generate power. Run-of-river and pumped-storage systems both play important roles in modern grids.

🌊

Tidal & Wave Energy

Marine technologies capture energy from tidal movements and ocean waves. Though still emerging, these technologies offer highly predictable output and great potential for coastal nations.

🌿

Biomass Energy

Organic material — agricultural waste, wood pellets, dedicated energy crops — is burned or converted to produce heat and electricity. When managed sustainably, biomass can be near carbon-neutral over its lifecycle.

Global Benefits

The shift to renewable energy brings advantages that extend far beyond simply reducing emissions. Economic, social, and environmental gains are all interlinked.

🌡️ Lower Emissions

Renewable energy dramatically reduces greenhouse gas output, helping limit global temperature rise and slowing the pace of climate change.

🏭 Energy Independence

Nations that generate their own renewable power reduce exposure to volatile fossil fuel markets and geopolitical supply disruptions.

💼 Job Creation

The renewable sector already employs over 10 million people worldwide, with solar and wind industries growing fastest in terms of new roles.

💰 Falling Costs

The cost of solar electricity has fallen by over 90% since 2010, making renewables the cheapest form of new power generation in most of the world.

Challenges

Despite rapid progress, the transition to renewable energy is not without obstacles. Understanding them is key to addressing them effectively.

Intermittency & Storage: Solar and wind only generate power when the sun shines or the wind blows. Without cost-effective storage — such as batteries or pumped hydro — grids can struggle to balance supply and demand around the clock.
High Upfront Costs: Although running costs are low, the initial capital required to build solar farms, wind turbines, or grid infrastructure can be a barrier, especially in lower-income countries.
Land & Environmental Use: Large-scale renewables require significant land area. Solar farms and wind turbines can affect local ecosystems and landscapes, making careful site selection and environmental review essential.
Grid Modernisation: Many existing electricity grids were designed for centralised, predictable fossil fuel generation. Integrating variable renewables requires significant investment in smart grid technology and transmission lines.

How You Can Help

Individual actions matter. While systemic change requires policy and industry commitment, everyday choices collectively shape the energy landscape.

  • Switch to a green energy tariff or supplier that sources renewable electricity
  • Install solar panels if you own your home — payback periods are now shorter than ever
  • Use energy-efficient appliances with high energy ratings (A or above)
  • Support candidates and policies that prioritise renewable investment
  • Reduce overall electricity consumption — turn off standby devices
  • Spread awareness in your community, school, or workplace
