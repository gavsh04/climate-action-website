Climate Policy Changes

How global agreements and national laws are shaping our response to the climate crisis — and what you can do about it.

Why Policy Matters

Climate change is already causing floods, droughts, and extreme weather around the world. Individual actions like recycling or cutting energy use help, but they are not enough on their own. We need strong government policies to make the big changes required.

Climate policy includes laws, targets, and funding plans that force industries and governments to cut emissions and switch to clean energy.

Flooding from extreme weather — a result of rising global temperatures (Source: Unsplash, royalty-free)

  • Global temperatures have already risen by 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels.
  • Without stronger policies, the world is heading for around 2.5°C of warming.
  • The poorest communities suffer most, even though they cause the least emissions.

The Paris Agreement

The Paris Agreement was signed in 2015 by 195 countries. Its goal is to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. Every country must publish a plan — called an NDC — showing how they will reduce emissions.

World leaders working together on climate agreements (Source: Unsplash, royalty-free)

  • Countries must update their plans every 5 years, each time with higher ambition.
  • Rich countries committed to giving $100 billion per year to help poorer nations go green.
  • At COP28 in 2023, nations agreed for the first time to move away from all fossil fuels.
The Paris Agreement is the most important climate treaty ever signed — but countries must do much more to meet its goals.

UK Climate Policy

The UK's Climate Change Act (2008) was the world's first legally binding national climate law. In 2019, the UK became the first major economy to set a net-zero emissions target by 2050.

UK offshore wind farms are central to reaching net-zero (Source: Unsplash, royalty-free)

  • New petrol and diesel cars will be banned from 2035.
  • The government plans to decarbonise the electricity grid by 2035.
  • The independent Climate Change Committee checks whether the UK is on track.
  • However, the CCC has warned the current plans are still not strong enough.

Challenges

There is a big gap between what governments promise and what they actually do. This is called the "implementation gap" and it is one of the biggest problems in climate politics today.

Fossil fuels continue to be used worldwide despite climate agreements (Source: Unsplash, royalty-free)

  • Fossil fuel companies spend millions lobbying against stronger climate laws.
  • Many countries have targets but no real plans to meet them.
  • Climate finance promised to developing countries is often not delivered.
  • The UK has approved new North Sea oil licences, which contradicts its net-zero goal.
Even if every country met its current promises, the world would still warm by 2.5°C — far above the safe 1.5°C limit.

What Students Can Do

You do not have to wait to graduate before influencing climate policy. Here are simple things you can do right now:

  • Write to your MP — ask them to support stronger climate laws.
  • Vote on climate — check a candidate's climate views before every election.
  • Join a campaign — groups like Friends of the Earth need student voices.
  • Push your university — ask for fossil fuel divestment and lower campus emissions.
  • Stay informed — read the UK Net Zero Strategy and follow COP news.
  • Talk about it — conversations with friends and family shift public opinion over time.
Student pressure helped push the UK to set its net-zero target. Your voice genuinely matters.
